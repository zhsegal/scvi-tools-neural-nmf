# Clonal Heterogeneity and Copy-Number Alterations in Cutaneous T-Cell Lymphoma: A Technical Briefing

## Executive Summary

Contemporary genomic and single-cell analyses have redefined the understanding of Cutaneous T-Cell Lymphoma (CTCL), particularly Mycosis Fungoides (MF) and Sézary Syndrome (SS). Contrary to the traditional model of a monoclonal mature T-cell origin, emerging evidence suggests that CTCL often originates from an immature precursor cell, resulting in significant intratumoral subclonal heterogeneity. While a dominant T-cell receptor (TCR) clone is typically present, it does not always represent a monolithic genetic entity. Instead, the malignant clone frequently harbors diverse copy-number alterations (CNAs) and chromosomal instabilities that evolve over time. Recurrent arm-level alterations, such as gains in chromosome 7 and losses in 10q (PTEN) and 17p (TP53), are linked to disease progression and therapeutic resistance. This document synthesizes recent findings on the interplay between TCR clonality, recurrent copy-number variations (CNVs), and the evolutionary dynamics of the malignant clone.

---

## 1. The Relationship Between the TCR Clone and CNV Signatures

The relationship between the TCR-defined clone and its genetic signature is complex. While TCR rearrangements are traditionally used as markers of monoclonality, deep sequencing reveals that "monoclonal" populations often hide significant genomic diversity.

### Coherence of the Recurrent CNV Signature
Research indicates that the dominant TCR clone does not always carry a single, coherent genetic signature across all cells. Instead, each malignant T-cell clone possesses an individual and often highly specific CNV pattern (Srinivas et al., 2024). 
*   **Early vs. Late Alterations:** In some cases, chromosomal duplications occur at an early stage of T-cell development *before* the completion of TCR rearrangements. For instance, trisomy of chromosome 7 (harboring the TCR$\beta$ locus) has been observed in cells with different TCR$\beta$ transcripts, suggesting the CNA predated the final TCR recombination (Hamrouni et al., 2019).
*   **Discordance in TCR Chains:** Malignant cells can share identical TCR$\gamma$ sequences while harboring different TCR$\alpha$ and $\beta$ sequences. This suggests that the initial transformation may occur in a precursor cell after TCR$\gamma$ rearrangement but before $\alpha/\beta$ recombination, allowing subsequent subclones to diverge genetically while appearing related via a single TCR marker (Hamrouni et al., 2019).

## 2. The Recurrent Chromosomal Arm-Level CNV Landscape

CTCL is characterized by a high burden of focal deletions and arm-level alterations that affect key regulatory pathways, including TCR signaling, JAK-STAT, and NF-$\kappa$B.

### Key Recurrent Alterations and Genetic Targets
Based on integrated genomic profiling and single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNAseq), several recurrent CNAs have been identified as central to CTCL pathogenesis:

| Chromosomal Region | Alteration Type | Key Target Genes / Associated Pathology |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chromosome 7** | Gain (Trisomy) | Harbors the TCR$\beta$ locus; evidence of early-stage duplication in precursor cells (Hamrouni et al., 2019). |
| **Chromosome 1p** | Gain | Specifically involving *ENO1* and *TNFRSF1B*; associated with metabolic adaptation (Srinivas et al., 2024). |
| **Chromosome 6** | Loss | Deletions involving MHC I and MHC II genes; contributes to immune evasion (Srinivas et al., 2024). |
| **9p21** | Loss | Affects *CDKN2A*; a recurrent tumor suppressor alteration in MF/SS (Crespi et al., 2026). |
| **10q** | Loss | Deletion of *PTEN*, *FAS*, and *NFKB2*; characteristic of leukemic MF and Sézary Syndrome (Srinivas et al., 2024). |
| **17p** | Loss | Affects *TP53*; linked to advanced disease stages and poor prognosis (Srinivas et al., 2024; Crespi et al., 2026). |

### Stage and Prognostic Associations
*   **Leukemic Involvement:** Deletions of 10q and 17p are more frequently observed in patients with leukemic involvement (SS) rather than skin-limited MF (Srinivas et al., 2024).
*   **Genomic Instability:** CTCL exhibits an unusual genomic architecture characterized by a high number of focal deletions (averaging 7.5 per sample). This instability is potentially driven by aberrant RAG-1/RAG-2 activity in precursor cells or environmental factors such as UV mutational signatures (Hamrouni et al., 2019; Crespi et al., 2026).

## 3. Intratumoral Heterogeneity and Clonal Evolution

The malignant T-cell population in MF/SS is not stationary; it undergoes continuous "phenotypic plasticity" and genetic shifting.

### Subclonal Heterogeneity Within the TCR Clone
Single-cell analysis confirms marked heterogeneity within a single TCR-defined clone. Malignant T cells within the same patient often form multiple sub-clusters with unique transcriptional and CNV profiles (Srinivas et al., 2024).
*   **Functional Phenotypes:** Subclones may diverge into different functional states, such as a "cytotoxic" phenotype (expressing *GZMA*, *NKG7*, *GNLY*) or a "naive/memory" phenotype (expressing *CD27*, *CCR7*, *IL7R*) within the same tumor (Srinivas et al., 2024).
*   **Evolutionary Transitions:** Using RNA velocity metrics, researchers have identified "stationary" vs. "transitioning" cell populations, indicating that some subclones are in a state of rapid transcriptional and genetic flux (Srinivas et al., 2024).

### Scaling of CNV Burden
Genomic burden in CTCL appears to scale with both time and therapeutic intervention:
*   **Disease Duration:** Patients with long-standing disease typically exhibit a higher number of genetic alterations (Srinivas et al., 2024).
*   **Treatment Impact:** Clonal evolution is accelerated by treatment. Specifically, ionizing radiation and multiple lines of systemic therapy are associated with increased genetic changes and CNV complexity (Srinivas et al., 2024).
*   **Clonal Expansion:** As the disease progresses from early patches to advanced tumors, there is a "progressive enrichment" of the dominant clone, accompanied by the accumulation of secondary genetic hits (Hamrouni et al., 2019).

## 4. Important Quotes with Context

> **"Malignant transformation takes place during early T-cell development giving rise to circulating premalignant clones, which home to the skin producing clinically apparent lesions."**
*   *Context:* This conclusion by Hamrouni et al. (2019) explains why diverse genetic signatures are often found within a single TCR-defined population, as the "hit" occurs before the TCR is fully locked.

> **"Each malignant T-cell clone possessed an individual CNV pattern... the extent of genetic alterations increases with the duration of the disease and the therapies applied, especially ionizing radiation."**
*   *Context:* From Srinivas et al. (2024), this highlights the dynamic nature of the CTCL genome and warns that clinical interventions may inadvertently drive further clonal divergence and complexity.

> **"The apparent inefficiency of GeneScan in detecting monoclonality... might in reality reflect polyclonality of lymphoma."**
*   *Context:* Hamrouni et al. (2019) argue that "false negative" clonality tests are actually biological evidence of the disease's clonal diversity rather than technical failures.

## 5. Actionable Insights

*   **Diagnostic Refinement:** Relying solely on TCR monoclonality may miss the underlying genetic complexity of CTCL. Clinical assessments should integrate CNV profiling (e.g., assessing 10q and 17p status) to better predict aggressive behavior, especially in erythrodermic patients.
*   **Therapeutic Targeting:** Since CTCL arises from an evolving system of subclones, targeting a single marker (like a mature T-cell antigen) may lead to relapse from "immature lymphoma precursor cells" that lack those specific markers. Multimodal or sequential therapies are necessary to address subclonal diversity.
*   **Monitoring Evolution:** Given that CNV burden scales with treatment (particularly radiation), longitudinal monitoring of the malignant clone's genetic architecture is essential to detect the emergence of resistant subclones.
*   **Immune Topography:** Future treatment selection should consider the "immune topography" of the tumor. High subclonal heterogeneity and specific CNV losses (like MHC genes on chromosome 6) are likely to dictate the success of checkpoint inhibitors and other immunotherapies.
